About

Hi! My name is Noah Truong and I am a second-year student at UCLA pursuing a bachelor's degree in Mechanical Engineering. My passion for STEM traces back to my early childhood when I would dream of being an inventor and accidentally break toys in the name of discovering how they function. This curiosity continued into middle and high school where I participated in the robotics team, working my way up to president and team captain for our FIRST Tech Challenge team. To this day, I mentor my old robotics team, giving back to the program that kickstarted my engineering career.

I have a demonstrated history of being a quick and independent learner with a passion and knack for problem-solving, teamwork, and education. Staring with my first 3D printer that I acquired at age 16, I began utilizing my knowledge to print useful objects. This eventually spiraled into a job at a local 3D printing company where I honed my repairing, troubleshooting, and post-processing skills. In the summer following my senior year of high school, I had the opportunity to work in the maker space at Northrop Grumman. Most recently, I spent the summer of 2020 as a remote Vehicle Engineering Intern on the Landing Gear and Brakes team at Northrop Grumman.

Outside of work, school, and my clubs, you can find me making things with one of my four 3D printers, working on my car, rock climbing, mountain biking, gaming, or enjoying a good series on Netflix.

Laser Cut Solar Car

Previous Next

This was my final project for my Introduction to Computer-Aided Design and Drafting class that I took in Spring '20. Unfortunately due to the COVID-19 pandemic, I was not able to build the solar car but I went through an extensive design process to optimize the design. The goals were to make a solar powered car out of laser cut acrylic, a motor, and a solar panel. During this project I made use of FEA to help shave unnecessary weight. Due to these optimizations, I was able to save 23.1% of the total weight of the car and achieved a 105/100 on this project.

3D Printed RGB LED Lamp

Previous Next

This is the final project I created before I left for college. I wanted to create something that would make use of all of the skills that I've learned throughout high school and that I could display in my dorm. I opted to design a custom lamp shade and the project eventually spiraled into a full blown lamp. I used WS2812B individually adressable LEDS so that I could make custom animations with my lamp. The lamp is powered by an ESP32 for future wireless control capabilities, but for now I control it with a button that I wired into the microcontroller.

Wrist Rest

Previous Next

This is a wrist rest that I created to go along with a keyboard that I had recently built. Because the keyboard was longer than my 3D printer, I had to split the wrist rest up into two pieces. I joined them with a soldering iron and super glue. Since I wanted the surface finish to be smooth, I sanded the wrist rest, used gap filling primer and repeated the process until all the surfaces were free from layer lines. Lastly, I painted the whole thing black and used resin to fill in the triangles to make it a little more comfortable.

This is a nutcracker prop replica from the popular sitcom, The Office(US). I first saw the nutcracker in Season 6, Episode 13 and I fell in love with the mechanical design. I couldn't find any 3D files or drawings online so I completely reverse engineered the nutcracker from screenshots of the show. Because I had to adapt the model to work with 3D printing, it is not 100% accurate to the one displayed in the show but the general shape and spirit is retained.

Spool Holder

Previous Next

I decided to create this spool holder because I received a damaged ESUN PLA+ filament spool for my 3D printer. Since the outer rim of the spool was damaged, it would not roll properly on the spool holder provided with my Prusa Mini. I used the printer for a couple days with a jerry rigged spool holder but decided that I wanted a more polished solution. I decided to design a spool holder with a few design requirements: 1. Must use no extra hardware beyond the hardware included with the original Prusa spool holder. 2. Must be able to support multiple spool sizes. 3. Must be easy to replace spools. The spool holder itself has two internally mounted bearings and features a threaded center with two threaded end caps that can be unscrewed completely to replace spools and also allow multiple widths of spools to be used.

DIY 3D Printer

Previous Next

This is the second of my four aforementioned 3D printers. I was looking for a new project and a challenge after getting my first 3D printer. I found these plans for a printer called the HyperCube Evolution on Thingiverse and I setout to build it. I had to figure out the BOM and source all the parts(an entire project by itself). I printed all the pre-designed parts from Thingiverse, built the printer, wired it, configured the firmware, and then calibrated it. The printer has been through several revisions, most recently, I designed mounts for upgraded parts. This included an updated gantry for a "Mosquito" style hotend rather than an E3D V6 and custom bed adjustment system.
